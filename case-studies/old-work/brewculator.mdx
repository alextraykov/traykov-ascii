## Summary

Brewculator started from a repeated personal problem: while brewing coffee, I kept switching between ratio notes, a calculator, a timer, and brewing references. That context switching made a simple ritual feel more fragile than it needed to be.

The app combined predefined brewing ratios, unit controls, and method-specific tips into a small mobile assistant for coffee enthusiasts.

![Brewculator ratio calculator screens](https://traykov.cc/photos/brewculator/brewculator-twophones-left.png)

## Problem

Coffee brewing rewards precision, but most casual workflows are full of small interruptions. The user has to remember the method, find the right coffee-to-water ratio, convert units, and keep the recipe in mind while preparing the actual brew.

The design goal was to make the calculation step fast enough to disappear.

## Research and framing

I looked at the existing coffee-app landscape and found pieces of the answer scattered across calculators, brewing guides, forums, and paid apps. The opportunity was not a huge platform. It was a focused utility that kept the most common decisions in one place.

![Sketchbook wireframes for Brewculator](https://traykov.cc/photos/brewculator/brewculator-sketchbook-wireframe.png)

The app centered on three decisions:

- Recipe selection first, because ratio choice changes everything else.
- Two connected inputs, so coffee and water update each other immediately.
- Unit controls in the calculator itself, because metric and imperial preferences vary by person.

## Interface decision

The core screen uses a segmented recipe selector, a paired calculator, and a compact tips section. The selected recipe is reinforced in both the control and heading, so the user always knows which ratio is active.

![Brewculator filtering controls](https://traykov.cc/photos/brewculator/brewculator-mockup-filtering-pills.png)

The calculator does the main work. If the user enters coffee, the app calculates water. If they enter water, it calculates coffee. This keeps the interaction flexible without asking the user to think about the formula.

```mermaid
flowchart LR
  Method[Choose brew method] --> Ratio[Load ratio]
  Ratio --> Input[Enter coffee or water]
  Input --> Convert[Convert selected units]
  Convert --> Output[Return paired amount]
```

## Onboarding

The onboarding flow handled two setup decisions: default brewing methods and preferred units. That made the first calculator session feel personal without adding configuration to the main screen.

![Brewculator onboarding flow](https://traykov.cc/photos/brewculator/onboarding-800x600.gif)

## Development

I used the project to learn SwiftUI and prototype the calculation model. The early prototype used simple get-set behavior: capture the active input, calculate against the selected ratio, and update the paired value.

![SwiftUI calculation prototype](https://traykov.cc/photos/brewculator/swiftui-calculation.gif)

## Outcome

Brewculator became a compact product-design case study about turning a niche personal frustration into a focused utility: fewer tools, fewer mental jumps, and more attention left for the actual brew.

## What I would sharpen now

- Add saved recipes and recent brews without turning the app into a diary.
- Make timer support method-aware instead of generic.
- Treat accessibility and one-handed use as first-pass constraints, not polish.
