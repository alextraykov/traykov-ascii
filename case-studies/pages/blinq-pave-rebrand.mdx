## Summary

The product that became Pave went through four names: Vibe UI, Flint, Blinq, and Pave.

That naming history matters because the product did not only need screens. It needed a design system and operating model that could survive changing identity.

My role started after the initial engineering incubation. I helped move the work from one-off product exploration into a design-led, token-governed product surface.

## Project frame

- Role: Lead Product Designer for AWE / Pave.
- Scope: product transition, app shell, design-system translation, accessibility guardrails, rebrand execution.
- Timeframe: December 2025 through April 2026, with related marketplace work continuing in June.
- Partners: product, engineering, brand, content, and design-system collaborators.

The archive supports launch facts around the nav going live on February 20, the April Pave launch milestone, and marketplace guided flows going live in June. It does not include measured Pave north-star outcomes like app creation completion or time-to-first-value.

## From brand pass to token system

The rebrand worked because style decisions moved into tokens and shared patterns before every screen became a custom patch.

The archive calls out one proof point: the rebrand shipped in one commit with semantic tokens, Pave Green accent, oatmeal surfaces, Pave logo variants, font stack changes, and zero component files touched.

![Pave styleguide and token capture.](/case-studies/pave-evidence/pave-styleguide-top-light.webp)

## Accessibility over literal brand fidelity

The strongest rebrand decision was a refusal.

Raw Pave Yellow `#FFFA00` had 1.11:1 contrast on white, unusable for text. The accessible variant moved to amber at 5.13:1. Raw Pave Red had 3.5:1 and was darkened to 5.47:1.

This is the kind of decision I want visible in the portfolio because it shows the system did not treat brand as permission to reduce legibility.

## Role-aware navigation

Pave needed different paths for builders, admins, and end users. Navigation could not only be a list of routes.

The app shell had exact constraints: 256/72 sidebar behavior, 40px nav items, 56px header, and theme application as a realm-level action rather than a casual cosmetic toggle.

![Navigation specification from the archive.](/case-studies/pave-evidence/refresh-navigation-spec.webp)

The role-aware navigation went live on February 20, 2026. Later Pave work kept extending the same question: who is acting, what surface do they need, and what should stay hidden until relevant?

## What is mine and what is not

This story needs clean attribution.

Brand identity and palette direction were owned with brand/design partners. My contribution was translating that direction into accessible tokens, app shell decisions, implementation-safe patterns, and launch-critical sequencing.
