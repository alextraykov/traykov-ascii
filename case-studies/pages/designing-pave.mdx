## Summary

Pave was Quickbase's bet that AI-assisted app building could move beyond prompt-to-prototype and become a practical way for enterprise teams to create usable software.

I was the sole product designer through the first six months of the MVP push. My work shaped the product foundation across the builder, chat loop, build activity, preview, authentication flow, navigation, theme selection, direct-edit exploration, planning artifacts, and credit-state patterns.

Pave [launched publicly on April 28, 2026](https://www.quickbase.com/news/press-releases/quickbase-announces-pave). Quickbase positioned it as a full-stack AI app builder for enterprise teams: people describe what they need, shape the generated app, and put it to work with roles, publishing, hosted infrastructure, and governance around it.

The design challenge was:

> Make AI app creation feel fast without making it feel reckless.

That meant the generated app could not be the only important moment. Users also needed to understand what was happening, stay connected to the output, recover from mistakes, and trust the product as more than a disposable demo.

## Why hard

Most AI app builders make first generation feel impressive. The user types a prompt, the system produces an app-shaped artifact, and the demo works.

The harder product problem starts one step later.

Business users still need to understand what was created, edit the output, preserve what is already working, invite collaborators, publish changes, and trust the product path toward real operational use. If chat disappears after generation, the product feels like an intake form. If canvas takes over completely, AI feels like an onboarding trick. If AI keeps regenerating everything, users lose confidence making small changes.

Pave's MVP had to hold three tensions at once:

- Speed vs. control: generation should feel fast, but users still need to see where they can intervene.
- Simplicity vs. enterprise reality: first-run experience needed to stay light while pointing toward roles, publishing, infrastructure, and governance.
- Exploration vs. launch: early product exploration was broader than what the first public MVP could responsibly ship.

I worked closely with a small senior team across product, engineering, architecture, executive stakeholders, marketing, brand, and content. My contribution centered on four areas:

- Foundational product model: how app creation, builder, chat, preview, navigation, and project state connected
- Core launch surfaces: authentication, builder, chat input, project builder layout, navigation, theme selector, and build-state components
- Product clarity: diagrams, state maps, behavior specs, edge cases, accessibility notes, and design QA
- Future-state exploration: planning, editing, configuration, billing, marketplace, and operational workflows that clarified where Pave could go after launch

Marketing, branding, and content shaped the final public-facing launch expression. My role was to establish the product foundation early enough that the team could converge, cut scope, and ship.

## Product strategy

I used four principles to keep the MVP coherent.

### Start with intent

The first interaction needed to begin with what the user wanted to build, not with a configuration sequence.

The desired posture was:

> Tell Pave what you need.

Not:

> Create a workspace, choose settings, configure an app, then begin.

That principle shaped the authenticated entry experience and the way the builder treated chat as a core product surface rather than a separate onboarding step.

### Keep chat and builder connected

The builder answered the most important interaction question in the MVP:

> Where does AI live once the user starts building?

I wanted the conversation and the app being created to feel like two views of the same object. The builder brought together chat input, app context, build activity, preview, theme selection, publish-oriented actions, and navigation back into the broader app shell.

The important design detail was not only that the page used a split layout. It was that neither side could dominate. Users needed enough room to talk to AI, but the app being created had to remain the anchor.

![January 2026 builder scaffold from the archive.](/case-studies/pave-evidence/scaffold-jan-2026-blinq.webp)

The split-pane model became the structural expression of the product thesis: AI should remain present as a collaborator, but the generated app should not disappear behind the conversation.

### Make AI activity visible

A spinner would not be enough.

When the system was building, users needed a sense that work was happening in a structured way. I designed build-state patterns and subagent cards to make system activity legible without turning the product into a log viewer.

The builder needed to communicate:

- Something is happening.
- The work has steps.
- The app is being shaped by the user's intent.
- The user can return to steering after the system finishes.

Too little activity feels opaque. Too much activity feels like fake technical theater. The narrow line was showing enough system activity to support trust without asking the user to read backend process as product value.

### Cut scope without breaking the thesis

The April 28 public launch forced hard prioritization. Early explorations covered deeper planning, editing, configuration, history, billing, marketplace, and operational workflows. Those explorations were useful because they clarified the long-term product direction, but they were too much for the first public MVP.

The real design work became reduction:

- Broad product possibility
- MVP product thesis
- Launchable interaction foundation
- Implementation-ready components

The question became:

> What must remain true for the MVP to still feel like Pave?

For me, the answer was:

- Users can start from intent.
- Chat and building feel connected.
- The app surface remains visible and controllable.
- Product state is explicit enough to trust.
- The experience has a credible path toward enterprise app creation.
- The product is polished enough to support a public launch.

## Builder loop

The builder carried the main product argument. It needed to avoid two failure modes: becoming just a chatbot, or becoming a visual editor where AI disappears after generation.

The core loop was:

1. User describes what they want.
2. Pave clarifies or plans when needed.
3. Build activity becomes visible.
4. Preview remains anchored.
5. User can inspect, adjust, and continue the conversation.

The loop mattered more than any single component. It let the product feel fast while still giving the user a visible object to reason about.

The January builder scaffold is useful evidence because it shows the product structure early: chat, preview, app chrome, input, and generation state treated as one workspace rather than separate screens. Later work refined the visuals, but the core product model was already present.

This also made mobile prioritization clearer. On small viewports, the builder input and active work needed priority over secondary monetization or navigation. Credit nudges, banners, and account surfaces could not be allowed to collapse the main creation loop.

## Planning before generation

Pave also needed a step between prompt and generated app. Without that, the product could feel fast but reckless: the system starts building before the user understands what it thinks it is building.

Planning introduced reviewable trust artifacts into the AI loop:

- Clarification when the prompt was ambiguous
- A plan object that could be revised through chat
- Workflow previews that showed triggers, conditions, actions, owners, and missing pieces
- Connection setup that stayed inside the building path
- A single approval moment before execution started

![PlanCard light-mode capture from the archive.](/case-studies/pave-evidence/plancard-light.webp)

Small copy decisions did real work here. `Approve & Run` was the right kind of explicit: it told the user execution starts now without turning the flow into approval theater.

The planning layer made conversation history more than a transcript. It became an audit trail of user intent, system interpretation, and the next action the product was about to take.

## Direct edit

Generated output needed a local editing model. If every correction routes through prompt, users can lose work that was already right.

Direct Edit answered a specific failure mode in AI builders:

> A small change should not require a risky regeneration.

The model used three layers:

- Selection grammar: hover, selected, dirty, and multi-selected states
- Controls: universal toolbar plus component-specific settings
- Staged changes: live preview before save or discard

![Direct Edit sequence archive.](/case-studies/pave-evidence/direct-edit-sequence.webp)

This gave small visual changes a safer path than prompt-only editing. `Make heading smaller` or `give this card less padding` should not risk changing the table below it. AI remained available for semantic changes, but deterministic edits needed a deterministic path.

The strongest detail was the save/discard boundary. Previewing a local change live is useful only if the user knows when it becomes the new baseline and how to recover if it was wrong.

## Credits as product state

Credits were not only a billing detail. In an AI builder, credits sit directly between user intent and system action.

If a limit appears only when work stops, the product feels unfair. If upgrade prompts appear too early or too loudly, the product feels like a funnel instead of a tool. Pave needed to answer practical questions in context:

- How many credits do I have?
- When do they renew?
- Why am I seeing this warning?
- What can I do now?
- Can I keep my work moving?

![Billing credit sequence from the archive.](/case-studies/pave-evidence/billing-sequence.webp)

The useful model was a four-state ladder: healthy, warning, low, depleted. Each state had to map across the header pill, chat card, nudge banner, pricing, checkout, account billing, and recovery actions.

The strongest pattern was inline recovery inside chat. If a user hit a limit while building, a modal would break the main loop. A chat card could explain the state and offer next steps without pretending the work context disappeared.

## Marketplace and governance

Marketplace work extended the same product model beyond a blank prompt. Users needed to discover reusable app starts, inspect them, adapt them, and publish within a governed enterprise context.

The important move was away from vague `Preview` behavior and toward real inspection: open the app start, understand what it includes, then fork it into the builder with context preserved.

![Marketplace sequence from the archive.](/case-studies/pave-evidence/marketplace-sequence.webp)

Marketplace also introduced policy-aware publishing. A fast realm could publish directly. A governed realm could require review. The CTA needed to respect that difference: `Submit for review` is not the same product promise as `Publish`.

This was post-launch ecosystem work, but it proved the same design posture could scale: make the next product action visible, preserve context, and keep enterprise control paths explicit.

## Executable product specs

Because the team was small and senior, static mockups were not enough.

The design needed to answer implementation questions:

- What states exist?
- What changes before, during, and after generation?
- How does navigation behave?
- Which components are reusable?
- Which parts are launch-critical?
- Which parts can be deferred?
- Where are the known gaps?

I used runnable prototypes and structured notes as part of the design work. The useful artifact was not a pile of screenshots. It was a product behavior model that made user goal, interaction state, component behavior, implementation note, and known gap visible.

That helped the team avoid treating design as a pile of screens. It made the product easier to reason about and helped implementation conversations stay grounded in behavior.

## Outcome

Pave launched publicly on April 28, 2026.

I am not sharing internal product metrics. The public impact I can safely describe is qualitative:

- Product impact: I established the prompt-first builder model where chat, build activity, preview, navigation, and product state worked together as one loop.
- Execution impact: design work made product behavior reviewable through prototypes, diagrams, implementation-oriented handoffs, and public-safe evidence artifacts.
- Strategic impact: the MVP clarified that AI-assisted app creation needed to become more understandable, controllable, and operationally useful after the initial generation moment.

## Reflection

The biggest lesson from Pave was that AI product design is not only about the generation moment.

The impressive demo is the easy part. The hard part is everything around it:

- Helping users understand what the system is doing
- Giving them enough control to trust the output
- Preserving the relationship between prompt and app
- Making product state visible
- Cutting scope without breaking the thesis
- Helping engineering understand design well enough to ship it

Using code helped make behavior concrete. It made states, motion, layout, copy, and component intent easier to evaluate. It also created a real tension: a design-intent prototype can look production-ready when it is not.

The better framing was:

> Prototype, not production architecture. A working design artifact that made behavior, state, and intent easier to implement.

Looking back, I would have created an even clearer shipped-versus-exploratory map earlier. Some explorations were intentionally broader than launch scope, and the team needed sharp boundaries between what we were shipping, what we were validating, and what we were using to clarify direction.

But the core product model held. Pave needed to feel fast, but not reckless. It needed to make AI feel powerful, but not opaque. It needed enough structure for a small team to move from ambiguity to a public MVP in six months.
