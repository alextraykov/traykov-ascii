## Summary

Synapse-Sys started with a simple frustration: when I am making sense of a messy topic, the useful part is rarely one isolated note. It is the relationship between two ideas.

Most AI tools make that relationship disappear into a chat response. I wanted to try a different model:

> connect two ideas, choose what the relationship should do, and get the result back on the canvas as something you can edit.

This is a pre-launch, solo prototype. It does not claim adoption or shipped business impact. The value of the case is the product judgment: how a loose AI concept became a coherent interaction model, and how that model held up once I had to design failure states, cost controls, graph mutation, visual hierarchy, sharing, exports, billing, and performance.

## Project Frame

- Role: solo product designer and design engineer, from product thesis through QA hardening.
- Product: an AI graph canvas for early synthesis work.
- Users: people turning raw material into structure, such as researchers, founders, strategists, designers, and technical leads.
- Evidence: working prototype, PRD, implementation specs, design audits, tests, launch-readiness notes, and 121 commits.
- Boundary: no launch or adoption claim. This is a zero-to-one product exploration case.

The audience for this case is a design hiring manager first and a design engineer second. The hiring-manager read is: I took an ambiguous AI idea, made the interaction concrete, and kept making product calls when the prototype exposed hard edges. The design-engineer read is underneath that: those product calls show up in rendering choices, data boundaries, server actions, budget rules, and failure contracts.

## The Product Bet

The first decision was what Synapse-Sys would refuse to be.

It was not a chatbot next to a whiteboard. It was not a global prompt box that throws text into a canvas. It was not automatic suggestions everywhere. Those patterns are easy to build, but they make the user's map feel like a place where AI leaves comments.

The sharper bet was executable edges. A user connects two nodes, chooses an operation such as expand, compare, synthesize, or summarize, and Synapse-Sys creates a result node between the original ideas. The answer becomes part of the graph. It can be moved, edited, connected, forked, or used as source material for another operation.

That gave the product a clear rule:

> if AI creates something, it must return as editable canvas material.

## The Moment It Got Real

The happy path was easy to demo. The hard part showed up when I asked what happens if the AI operation fails halfway through.

The first implementation felt fast because it created a pending node and replacement edges before the AI result was ready. On a perfect run, that looked great. On a bad run, it could corrupt the user's thinking surface. A provider timeout, budget failure, network error, or failed edge insert could leave the original edge gone, a skeleton node stuck on the canvas, or replacement edges pointing at the wrong thing.

That was the moment Synapse-Sys stopped being a neat demo and became a product problem.

I changed the contract:

- Show pending feedback locally so the canvas still feels alive.
- Keep the persisted graph unchanged while the AI call is running.
- If the AI call fails, remove the local pending state and restore the original edge.
- If it succeeds, commit the graph transformation in one server-side transaction.

The transaction inserts the result node, deletes the original edge, creates `source -> result`, and creates `result -> target`. The user still sees motion and progress, but the database only changes when the full operation can complete cleanly.

## Canvas Architecture Was A Product Decision

A pure canvas would have made graph geometry easier and node editing worse. A pure DOM surface would have made rich node cards easier and edge rendering worse.

I chose a hybrid model:

- Konva renders the grid, edges, hit regions, connection previews, and selection geometry.
- HTML renders the node cards above the canvas using the same viewport transform.

That split let nodes behave like interface objects. They can support text selection, edit states, resize handles, tooltips, buttons, context menus, and accessible markup. Edges can behave like graph geometry, with orthogonal routing, labels, invisible hit regions, z-ordering, bridge arcs, and zoom-compensated strokes.

The cost is synchronization. Pan, zoom, drag, culling, selection, resize, hit-testing, and side-panel focus all have to agree about the same coordinate system.

## Edges Became The Interface

Once the edge became the command surface, edge quality became product quality.

Dense maps get unreadable quickly. Straight lines look simple until the canvas fills up. Clever bundled routes look technical, but they make individual relationships harder to follow. I chose readability over visual cleverness.

The edge system became its own design language:

- Orthogonal paths instead of arbitrary diagonals.
- Port selection based on source and target geometry.
- Minimum elbow stubs so arrows do not sit flush against cards.
- Obstacle avoidance around intermediate nodes.
- Shared corridors for related horizontal paths.
- Bridge arcs where paths cross.
- Operation-specific dash patterns.
- Labels that avoid bridges and other labels.
- Priority rules for selected, hovered, operation, and plain edges.

One discarded direction was a PCB-style staircase fan-out with shared ports. It was the more impressive engineering sketch. It was also harder to read. The chosen system spends more space so relationships stay selectable, labelable, and understandable.

## Suggestions Had To Stay Provisional

AI suggestions are useful until they become clutter. If every related concept becomes a permanent node, the canvas stops feeling authored by the user. It becomes a cleanup job.

So suggestions became ghost nodes. A ghost is visible, positioned, and connected to the selected idea, but it is not committed to the graph. The user can accept it, dismiss it, or ignore it. If accepted, it becomes a real node. If ignored, it disappears without leaving debris.

That small product rule created real state work:

- Cache suggestions by source node.
- Track ghost anchors.
- Place ghosts around the selected node.
- Displace nearby nodes without permanently moving them.
- Materialize accepted suggestions optimistically.
- Pan the viewport enough to reveal a new branch.

The principle was simple:

> AI should widen the user's peripheral vision, not take over the map.

## The Product Had To Survive Outside The Canvas

The canvas was the heart of the product, but a tool is not shippable just because the main interaction works once.

Users need accounts. They need recovery links that work. They need budgets, share links, exports, billing, settings, error states, security boundaries, and observability. Each wrapper surface forced a practical product question:

- Who owns this canvas?
- Who can mutate this node?
- What happens when the user runs out of AI operations?
- Can the user recover their account?
- What happens if a shared link expires?
- Can the billing provider change without rewriting the product?
- Can failures be inspected after launch?

The supporting layer included Supabase SSR auth, protected routes, Drizzle schema for canvases and graph objects, server-action authorization helpers, share-link expiry and fork behavior, JSON/Markdown/Obsidian/FigJam/GDPR exports, AI usage events, monthly operation budgets, Redis-backed rate limits, PostHog, Sentry, settings, pricing, profile, onboarding, and public share pages.

## Trust Lived Server-Side

The architecture followed the product loop. The client could show pending state. The server owned durable mutation, budget enforcement, auth checks, prompt construction, provider calls, and output validation.

The important boundary was not the stack itself. It was where the product put trust.

That could have turned into a rewrite. It did not, because billing already sat behind a provider interface. The active provider became configuration, while pricing, plan state, and AI budgets kept talking to the same product contract.

## AI Needed Economics, Not Just Prompts

An AI product has to balance quality, speed, cost, and safety at the same time.

Synapse-Sys could not treat model calls as one generic box. A quick ghost suggestion does not need the same quality bar as a synthesis result. URL extraction needs SSRF protection. Streaming work needs cancellation, heartbeat handling, and server-derived prompts. Every operation needs budget enforcement.

## The Visual Identity Had To Calm Down

The first visual direction was loud on purpose: black background, phosphor green, VT323, pixel shadows, scanline energy, and terminal boot behavior. It gave Synapse-Sys a memorable shape fast.

It also created a real risk. The interface could become more interested in its own atmosphere than in the user's ideas.

Audits made that obvious. The canvas needed stronger hierarchy. Node content needed easier reading. Green needed secondary tones. Glow needed restraint. CTAs needed better contrast. The product had to keep its terminal identity without making every surface shout at the same volume.

The system changed in practical ways:

- Secondary green introduced.
- Glow intensity reduced.
- Tooltips added.
- Node wrapping and truncation revisited.
- Side-panel word wrapping fixed.
- Theme first-paint issues corrected.
- Mobile touch foundations added.
- Node passes shifted toward content-first scanability.

## Performance Was About Trust

Canvas performance is not only frame rate. It is whether the user still believes they know where they are.

If panning stutters, if zoom jumps away from the cursor, if selected nodes disappear during culling, or if pinch gestures fight the browser, the map stops feeling stable.

The performance work focused on interaction fidelity:

- Cursor-stable wheel zoom.
- Trackpad pan and zoom behavior closer to Figma expectations.
- Viewport culling with world-space bounds.
- Quantized viewport keys to reduce recomputation.
- Larger touch buckets.
- Render-set freezing during active gestures.
- Forced rendering for selected, focused, and side-panel nodes.
- Pinch and drag throttling.
- Session-only positions during gestures.

The landing hero sphere had its own version of the same problem. It needed to feel alive without becoming expensive, so it moved through crash fixes, lookup-table colors, scratch buckets, frame limiting, and reduced-motion behavior.

## How I Used LLMs

Synapse-Sys was built with heavy LLM assistance. I do not want to hide that, and I also do not want to overstate what it means.

The models helped with execution. They did not own the product.

My work was defining the thesis, interaction model, architecture boundaries, QA criteria, hardening priorities, and review process. The important part was creating a constrained environment where LLMs could move quickly without casually changing the product:

- Architecture rules defined stack, boundaries, and product constraints.
- Phase plans broke the work into scoped implementation steps.
- Product and interaction specs defined behavior before code.
- Design audits turned weak points into implementation waves.
- Server-action rules prevented casual client-side data mutation.
- AI guardrails kept model calls server-side and structured.
- Tests protected routing, culling, budget behavior, import validation, share behavior, and edge-operation behavior.

The skill I want this case to show is not "I prompted code into existence." It is that I can turn product judgment into explicit constraints, use AI to accelerate implementation, and still own the product when the system gets messy.

## What Broke

The most useful failures exposed weak product contracts:

- Edge operations could leave bad graph state.
- Operation picker state raced.
- AI budget enforcement missed some operation paths.
- Password recovery ran into real-world email-scanner hash-link behavior.
- The terminal aesthetic sometimes reduced hierarchy instead of strengthening identity.

Each failure forced a clearer product rule, not just a local fix.

## What This Proves

This case proves that I can take an ambiguous AI product idea and make it concrete enough to evaluate. It shows interaction modeling, visual taste under restraint, design-system thinking, product hardening, and enough engineering depth to expose tradeoffs early.

It also proves a boundary. Synapse-Sys is strongest as a zero-to-one design-engineering case. It is not yet a shipped-impact case. It does not include real customer adoption, retention, usability findings, activation metrics, generated-node reuse data, or ghost-node acceptance data.

If I kept pushing it, I would focus on fewer things, not more features:

- Measure the activation loop: first node, first edge, first AI operation, first branch, first share.
- Turn public share pages into an acquisition loop.
- Move onboarding state server-side so it survives devices.
- Close RLS migration drift.
- Add integration tests around database, RLS, and webhook paths.
- Keep reducing visual noise so the terminal identity supports content.
- Build a small template gallery before adding more AI operations.

The product does not need more magic before it needs clearer measurement and trust.

## What This Shows

Synapse-Sys is not interesting because it has a canvas, uses AI, or has a lot of commits.

It is interesting because one product decision created a chain of design and engineering consequences:

> Make relationships executable.

That forced a real product system: spatial interaction, graph topology, AI operation design, edge routing, provisional suggestions, transaction safety, usage economics, billing, security, performance, and launch surfaces.

This is the kind of design-engineering work I want to keep doing: defining product logic, making the interaction model concrete, using code to expose the hard parts early, and staying with the system until the original idea can survive real constraints.
