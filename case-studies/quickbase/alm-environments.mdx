## I arrived with the wrong language

`Dev -> Test -> Prod` is perfectly ordinary if you ship software for a living. Quickbase builders are not all software teams, which meant the feature arrived carrying a vocabulary problem before I had drawn much of the flow.

I had spent years around Solutions Hub, versioning, rollback, failure states, Sandboxes, and Merge Map. That history was useful, but it also made the model feel more obvious to me than it had any right to feel. The job was to keep the safety of a release pipeline without requiring a business builder to cosplay as DevOps.

I led the research planning, terminology work, workflow design, approval and lock states, QA, and the measurement frame. Some of the earlier 2022–2024 explorations never shipped as one neat product. They were still the pile of failed and partial thinking that Environments grew from.

## My role

- Role: Lead Product Designer and research lead for ALM / Solutions.
- Timeframe: August 2025 through January 2026 design hubs and QA sign-off.
- Partners: Product management, engineering, content design.

## I tested comprehension, not preference

The research separated IT administrators from business builders, then asked each person to create an environment, submit changes for review, and explain what had happened in their own words. I was not looking for the nicest label in a preference test. I wanted to know whether the model survived without hints.

If somebody reached for “editing” while the product said “Dev”, the product did not get to win the argument because our terminology sounded more official.

## The amount of ceremony had to follow the risk

Not every promotion deserved the same procession of confirmations. I designed per-environment deployment rules so a team could keep low-risk moves light and put the stricter approval around production, where it belonged.

The model separated environment admin, solution admin, reviewer, contributor, and auditor. Permissions were part of the main flow, not an administrative appendix.

### I started with the locked states

Environments was partly an Enterprise capability, so some people would meet the paywall or permission model before they met the happy path. I designed those states early. A disabled control with no explanation turns governance into a product shrug.

![Environment selection states: default and plan-limited variants.](/case-studies/quickbase/alm-environments-selection.webp)

### Finding an old deployment without building filter soup

For deployments, I used a search box with `status:`, `user:`, `from:`, `to:`, and date filters instead of letting the page grow a dropdown for every possible question.

The accessibility notes were not launch polish. Governance tools get audited by actual people, sometimes on assistive tech. Status needed text and shape, not colour alone.

## People used the longer path

Measured adoption, from the fixed July 20, 2025 to July 19, 2026 Pendo window:

- 160 accounts created environments.
- 90 accounts published or deployed through the native environments UX.
- The visitor funnel from environment creation to publishing changes converted at 40.6%: 313 people started and 127 went on to publish, typically across several sessions.
- Launch-quarter structure instrumentation captured only eight account choices: six chose DEV -> TEST -> PROD and two chose DEV -> PROD. Directional, not an adoption ratio I would generalise.
- Version restore and review-changes flows were in real use, but at smaller counts.

The small launch-quarter preference for the longer path suggests some teams wanted the extra safety step. It is not proof that every deployment was understood or successful.

My scorecard also named environment-creation completion, deploy success, failure-investigation time, CES, and CSAT. Those remain targets. The Pendo table confirms usage, not whether somebody completed the job cleanly.

## The measurement hole I left

I wrote a respectable scorecard and did not get the success/failure event split instrumented before GA. So I can say 160 accounts created environments and 90 published or deployed through the native UX. I cannot calculate the success rate I said mattered.

That is the irritatingly ordinary failure here: the measurement plan existed, looked responsible, and still could not answer one of its own questions.
