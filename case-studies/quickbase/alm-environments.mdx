## Summary

Quickbase Environments brought Dev -> Test -> Prod release governance to a product used by business builders, not only developers.

The design problem was not simply making a deployment flow usable. It was translation. People needed the safety of CI/CD without being forced to think in CI/CD language.

My role covered research planning, terminology validation, workflow design, approval and lock-state modeling, QA, and the measurement frame used to judge the feature after launch.

## Project frame

- Role: Lead Product Designer and research lead for ALM / Solutions.
- Timeframe: August 2025 through January 2026 design hubs and QA sign-off.
- Partners: Product management, engineering, content design.
- Evidence source: downloaded portfolio archive, Pendo outcome table, Jira and Figma evidence.

## The hard part

Ask a business builder to move work from "Dev" to "Prod" and some freeze. Those are not always their words.

So I treated terminology as a product surface. The research plan split IT/admin personas from business builders and asked people to create an environment, submit changes for review, and explain what just happened in their own words.

The useful signal was not preference. It was comprehension. If a builder reached for "editing" where the UI said "Dev," that was a design fact.

## Decisions

### Name for the person using it

The research plan measured whether builders understood environment purpose, creation, and promotion without hints. The point was to catch vocabulary mismatch early enough to change the model, not decorate a finished one.

### Let governance scale with risk

Not every promotion deserves the same ceremony. I designed per-environment deployment rules so a team could keep low-risk moves light while requiring stronger approval around production.

The model supported separation of duties: environment admin, solution admin, reviewer, contributor, auditor. That matters in a governance feature because permission design is part of the product, not an admin appendix.

![Copy production data flow with data-copy choices, approval gating, and plan-tiered environment behavior.](/case-studies/quickbase/alm-copy-production-data.webp)

### Design locked state first

Environments was partly an Enterprise capability, so plan-limited states were real product states. I framed those as legible upgrade and permission moments rather than dead ends.

![Environment selection states: default and plan-limited variants.](/case-studies/quickbase/alm-environments-selection.webp)

### Make deployments searchable and auditable

For the deployments surface, I specified a token-aware search box instead of a stack of dropdowns: `status:`, `user:`, `from:`, `to:`, and date filters.

The accessibility notes were not polish. Governance tools get audited by real people, sometimes on assistive tech. Status needed text and shape, not color alone.

## Outcome

Measured adoption, from Pendo trailing 12 months:

- 134 accounts created environments.
- 77 accounts published or deployed through the native environments UX.
- Builders selected DEV -> TEST -> PROD over DEV -> PROD by roughly 3:1.
- Version restore and review-changes flows were in real use, but at smaller counts.

The scorecard I defined also included rate targets: environment creation completion, deploy success, failure investigation time, CES, and CSAT. Those stay labeled as targets because the Pendo usage table confirms adoption, not pass/fail success rates.

## What I would tighten

The measurement frame was right, but I would instrument success and failure event splits before GA. A benchmark that cannot be computed becomes a promise without teeth.
