## On July 22, I started deleting my own design

Connection Central is where Quickbase admins work out which systems, connections, tables, and pipelines are touching company data. The first version, designed by Johnny Lee, already exposed a lot of that information. My v2 work in 2024 was supposed to make the relationships easier to reason about.

Research gave me a better information architecture and, naturally, enough good ideas to miss the release date. On July 22 I removed the richer accordion and popover detail from the MVP because I had designed more than engineering could responsibly build before GA.

## My role

- Role: Lead Product Designer.
- Scope: research, IA, interaction design, MVP definition, Pendo strategy.
- Team: PM Stasha Markovska, engineering lead Dimitar Arnaudov, co-research with Lisa Sawyer.
- Launch: GA on December 17, 2024.

## Thirteen architects rearranged the problem

Connection Central v1 was informative but sprawling. Builders could see the objects; following what was wired to what was harder.

Before drawing a new structure, I ran a Structured Association survey with 13 Solution Architects. They were the closest proxy we had for power builders living inside integrations every day.

The pattern was consistent: people thought in resources and impact, not in our internal object plumbing. I had been looking at a system diagram. They were asking, “if I touch this connection, what breaks?” Fair.

## Keeping the map while inspecting one thing

I organised the page around resources. People needed to see what a connected system exposed, where each resource appeared, and which pipelines depended on it.

![Connection Central v2 resources-first IA flow.](/case-studies/quickbase/cc-v2-ia-flow.webp)

The two-pane model kept the resource map visible while an admin inspected detail. Losing the whole map to read one connection would have reproduced the same problem in a cleaner layout.

![Two-pane resources detail model.](/case-studies/quickbase/cc-resources-detail-two-pane.webp)

## What survived the cut

Engineering had a hard GA date and limited UI capacity late in the work. The useful question was no longer which version I preferred; it was which decision would be expensive to reverse later.

I kept the resources-first IA and the two-pane control-room shape. The accordion and popover detail could come later. Shipping the wrong organising model and decorating it afterwards would have been a much nastier problem.

![Pipelines tab in the Connection Central hub.](/case-studies/quickbase/cc-pipelines-list.webp)

## Then we skipped the beta

On November 6, 2024, I aligned with the PM to go straight to GA to hit the H2 date. There was no beta. This is the sort of sentence that looks decisive in a portfolio and feels considerably less elegant while making the call.

That made post-launch measurement more important, so I authored the v2 Pendo strategy: new-feature guide, onboarding walkthrough, feedback poll, and help update.

What I can still verify from the project record:

- GA on December 17, 2024.
- Research-led IA grounded in n=13 Solution Architect survey.
- Follow-up backlog tied to deferred survey needs.

## The read-back arrived later

The first archive did not include the Pendo adoption export. A later fixed-window pull did: from July 20, 2025 to July 19, 2026, 3,110 accounts and 8,436 visitors used the Connection Central page. Of the people who opened Connection Central through the measured entry point, 62.7% went on to use one of its tabs. I leave raw event volume out because automated activity contaminated that part of the stream; the account and visitor counts are the honest measures.

That read-back proves reach and navigation, not whether the resources-first model prevented mistakes. I would still protect the date. I would also fight harder for five moderated pre-GA sessions with the same architects. Pendo can tell me where people went after launch; it cannot tell me why a builder hesitated over a resource filter or misread a status. Skipping that evidence was the debt attached to the schedule call, and the debt did not vanish because we shipped.
