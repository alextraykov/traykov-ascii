## I had become part of the routing system

Requests came from several business units. Core and Pave used different design systems. Designers worked across time zones and unfamiliar product domains. Somewhere in that mess I became one of the places context went to live.

At first this felt useful. I knew which work was moving, who needed a review, why a decision had been made, and which request could wait. It was also a fairly stupid operating model if the team needed me in the room to recover any of it.

So the leadership work became less about having answers and more about getting intake, critique, onboarding, hiring, and weekly decisions out of my head and into a rhythm other people could use. I still did hands-on product work. This was the machinery around it.

I have left out review contents, candidate details, private feedback, internal URLs, and names. The artifacts matter here; the confidential material inside them does not belong in a portfolio.

## What I was responsible for

- Scope: Core platform work plus AWE / Pave.
- Systems: Bebop 2 for Core and the Pave library for the AI app builder.
- Domain ownership: Pipelines design ownership, plus adjacent ALM / Environments and platform work.
- Team operations: 15+ substantial weekly updates across 2025-2026.
- People leadership: design hiring ownership, candidate funnel ownership, review/ramp responsibility for roughly five designers.
- Privacy: review contents and names are excluded.

## Slack was not an intake system

Cross-BU requests had a habit of becoming Slack negotiation. Whoever was online inherited the pressure, and the loudest recent request could make half-built work look mysteriously less important.

I owned a central lead-level intake so requests could be sequenced, rejected, redirected, or staffed without every designer renegotiating priority alone.

The Connection Central v2 Feature Design Tracker is a good example. It used request type, priority, design status, decision date, and follow-up state to keep the work visible. We used it to make decisions, not simply to document them afterwards.

The split was deliberately plain:

- Feature-level work routes through intake.
- Fit-and-finish work moves onto Jira stories.
- Priority conflicts route through design leadership instead of forcing individual designers to drop half-built work because the loudest request arrived last.

## “Ready” could not keep meaning “looks good to me”

For ALM Business Builder, I wrote down the process the team would use instead of handling each request differently.

It set expectations around:

- jobs-to-be-done framing
- research-risk and prioritization
- milestone setting
- deliverable expectations
- accessibility checks
- design-system reference
- prototyping
- specs
- testing

It was a checklist, yes. But it stopped “ready” from changing meaning depending on who happened to be reviewing. Quality could still involve judgment; it just could not hide behind taste.

## Critique for people who were asleep

The 2023 Innovation Weekly Critique happened across time zones, which meant a live meeting alone would quietly turn geography into hierarchy. It had clear preparation, an async fallback in `#ux-critique`, and a rule to keep decisions visible instead of moving them into DMs. We later recorded critiques so designers in other time zones could catch up asynchronously.

The current practice has three modes:

- Fast Pass.
- Domain Share.
- Formal critique.

Over time it settled into a pre-read, visible questions, async participation, and a written record of why we made a decision. None of this is exotic. That was rather the point: a critique practice should survive a calendar conflict.

## Helping new designers learn the domain

I created structured domain onboarding material so new designers could understand the domain without booking a chain of one-off calls with me.

On July 2, 2026 I shared a `Pipelines - Design Onboarding` document for Nikola’s first weeks in the domain. It covered the team, its goals, active work, and the vocabulary I had previously explained repeatedly in calls. Writing it once was less heroic and considerably more useful.

## Hiring and looking after the candidate experience

As UX Team Lead, I owned open design requisitions, the Senior Product Designer funnel, the take-home assignment, candidate coordination, panel feedback, and candidate experience.

When a candidate hit a technical problem, I filed `SUPPORT-18624` and followed it through. It is an unglamorous artifact, but it says more about ownership than a paragraph about “candidate experience” would.

The contents of reviews are sensitive, so I do not reproduce them here.

## Keeping a weekly rhythm

Across 2025 and 2026 I wrote more than 15 substantial weekly updates linking the work back to Figma, PRDs, Jira epics, and domain priorities. Fifteen updates are not an operating transformation. They did stop a useful slice of the work from becoming oral history.

## What became visible

The practices left a record of:

- what work is actually in flight
- who owns it
- what the review path is
- which requests are out of scope
- how new designers ramp without private context
- how candidate and review processes stay human without leaking confidential detail

## What I still cannot prove

I can point to trackers, critique formats, onboarding documents, hiring artifacts, support follow-through, and more than 15 weekly updates. I cannot yet show that a designer ramped two weeks faster or that critique cycles fell by a measurable amount.

Ramp time, review cycles, intake resolution, and designer sentiment would tell me whether the machinery actually improved the team, rather than merely producing very organised evidence that I had been busy.
