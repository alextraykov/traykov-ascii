## The dangerous bit looked like a dropdown

A Quickbase builder can authorise Slack, Shopify, QuickBooks, Gmail, Snowflake, or an internal data source, then let Pipelines run against it without being a developer. The scary parts are not always dressed like scary parts. Sometimes it is a sandbox/production selector tucked into a connection form.

I worked on Pipelines for three years: the reusable OAuth anatomy, AI-generated steps, Bulk Trigger, and a Jinja code mode. I began by treating trust mostly as an explanation problem - make the flow clear enough and people will understand it. The work kept correcting that. Trust also came from where the product stopped, what it refused to commit, and whether a user could inspect the consequence before it touched real data.

### My part in it

- Role: Lead Product Designer across three years of Pipelines work.
- Scope: reusable connection patterns, AI-assisted creation, high-volume automation, and expert configuration.
- Product rule: AI could propose and populate work, but never auto-commit it.
- After launch: the AI generation flow reached 1,113 accounts in the fixed 365-day Pendo window ending July 19, 2026.

## Connecting an account

Every provider asks for its own peculiar thing: OAuth scopes, store URLs, sandbox/production choice, service accounts, or a permissions ladder long enough to make anyone click through on instinct.

Designing a bespoke screen for each connector would have made every new integration a fresh argument. I built one anatomy and gave provider-specific complexity a defined place inside it.

![OAuth connect anatomy for Pipelines.](/case-studies/quickbase/pipelines-oauth-connect-anatomy.webp)

Permissions were written in plain language instead of raw scope strings, because the person authorising a business workflow is often not an OAuth engineer. Sandbox versus production became a first-class choice, because hiding it in settings is how somebody discovers the environment model after corrupting the real books. Lovely.

## Letting AI suggest, but not commit

The Pipelines AI creation flow shipped as IN-17508. A builder describes a step, the model proposes a sequence and populates the fields - and then stops. Every field remains reviewable and editable.

Earlier experiments with AI Product Help, Channels, and Console had already dragged in everything the demo politely ignored: loading, no answer, usage limits, timeouts, permissions, and recovery. I had enough evidence by then that a confident result was not permission to commit it.

![AI step creation flow, from recommendation through populated fields.](/case-studies/quickbase/pipelines-ai-step-creation-flow.webp)

Measured adoption from the fixed July 20, 2025 to July 19, 2026 Pendo window:

- Generate Steps with AI: 1,113 accounts, 1,948 visitors, 4,047 events.
- Populate all steps: 1,033 accounts, 1,910 visitors, 4,994 events.

## Designing for thousands of records

Pipelines originally fired one row at a time. That model gets silly when somebody needs to move thousands of records.

I worked on the Bulk Trigger UX and its feedback loop, including the naming cleanup. “Bulk” and “Batch” had been used loosely enough that people could discuss the same feature and mean different behaviour. The terminology work was boring, slightly annoying, and load-bearing.

![Bulk Trigger flow through multi-record automation.](/case-studies/quickbase/pipelines-bulk-trigger-flow.webp)

Measured adoption in the archive:

- On New Bulk Event Trigger: 60 accounts and 76 visitors.

Engineering owned the performance improvements, so I do not attribute those results to my design work here.

## Making room for advanced builders

The visual editor made Pipelines approachable. It also became a ceiling for people who knew exactly what they wanted to write.

For power users, I designed a Jinja code-mode editor and the lock/disable rules needed so code mode and visual mode would not corrupt each other.

![Pipelines code-mode editor spec.](/case-studies/quickbase/pipelines-code-mode.webp)

Supporting both paths created ugly rules: when code mode owns the expression, visual controls have to lock or disable so the two representations do not quietly corrupt each other. It doubled a class of edge cases. Removing code mode would have made the product tidier by frustrating the builders most likely to hit its limits.

## What the numbers do and do not say

The AI generation flow reached 1,113 accounts in the fixed 365-day window. Bulk Trigger's `On New Bulk Event` reached 60 accounts. Those numbers show real use; they do not prove that my design caused the adoption, and the performance work on Bulk Trigger belonged to engineering.

The thread across OAuth, AI creation, Bulk Trigger, and code mode was less poetic than “designing trust”. Put the dangerous choice where people can see it. Let AI fill fields, then make it wait. Give advanced builders an escape hatch, even when it makes the state model uglier. I learned most of that after the happy path had already looked finished.
