## Executive Summary

This work started as **Blinq Design Playground** on 2026-01-27: a runnable React/Vite design-intent prototype with a tightly controlled sidebar, navigation model, theme application flow, and token vocabulary. Over the next month it became a full product prototype: notifications, template editing, role-based home views, permissions/settings/account pages, login flows, dark mode, motion affordances, screenshot discipline, and early agent guardrails.

In March 2026, the work shifted from a prototype to a **repeatable design delivery environment**. The repo became VibeDesign, added branch previews, preview banners, CI/deploy hardening, handoff templates, QA surfaces, and content/process rules. That turned the repository into a system for reviewing design through working URLs, not static mocks.

In April 2026, the product became **Pave**. The Blinq to Pave rebrand happened through tokens, logo, docs, and typography. Around the same time, the product depth expanded: billing and credits, credit packs, plan mode, ClarifyCard, visual editor/inspector work, multi-select, connections, workflow vision, and dark-mode defaults.

From late April through June 2026, the work matured into a coherent AI app-builder story: chat-driven workflow generation, diagram preview/revise, mobile responsiveness, connection setup, component-level direct edit, stage-aware suggestions, guided preview kit, marketplace publishing/review flows, onboarding funnels, template detail pages, app forking, Handoff Hub, component specs, and reliable deep links.

The portfolio story is not just "I designed screens." It is:

> I built a design-intent product prototype and the AI-assisted environment around it: token-governed, branch-previewed, documented, handoff-ready, and broad enough to express an entire AI app-builder product vision from onboarding to marketplace publishing.

## Portfolio Timeline - Expanded Design Context

### 1. 2026-01-27 to 2026-01-30 - Establishing the Design Playground

**What I built:** the first Blinq Design Playground, the main sidebar/navigation shell, logical nav groupings, the theme dropdown, and the first two-step "apply theme to realm" confirmation flow.

**What I designed in detail:**

- A precise app-chrome contract: `256px` expanded sidebar, `72px` collapsed rail, `40px` nav items, `16px` icons, `14px` nav type, `56px` header, `12px` gaps, and explicit sizing for app selector, chevrons, section toggles, and nav buttons.
- A navigation IA that grouped the product into clearer zones instead of a flat list. The early refactor is important because it set up the later product surface expansion: Builder, projects, settings, permissions, themes, templates, and account pages all needed a coherent shell.
- A theme-application interaction that treated "theme changes" as realm-level changes, not cosmetic toggles. The two-step confirmation flow made the risk visible: applying a theme affects more than the current page.
- A warm visual direction with lighter menu hovers, moving the prototype away from raw default UI and toward a deliberate design system surface.

**Portfolio angle:** this phase shows the prototype starting with layout discipline, not feature sprawl. The earliest commits are unusually specific about dimensions because the goal was to make the UI's structural grammar concrete and repeatable.

**Evidence commits:** `d636a36`, `c1051e8`, `3ac99b6`, `07841bf`, `a077fb0`, `37581eb`, `ca21580`, `7daa65e`, `b1f4cb3`, `1e43124`.

### 2. 2026-02-02 to 2026-02-10 - Core Surfaces, Tokens, and Motion Signals

**What I built:** Notifications, Template Editor updates, dark-mode fixes, token renaming from `globals.css` to `variables.css`, preview-panel badge motion, and an animated `SubagentBubble`.

**What I designed in detail:**

- The first notification/template-editing relationship: notifications were not only rows in a table, but objects connected to editable content templates.
- A stronger token vocabulary by renaming global CSS to `variables.css` and tightening the idea that visual choices should be routed through named variables.
- Dark-mode corrections early in the product life, so the prototype did not treat dark mode as a late skin.
- Preview-panel badge motion as an early example of state signaling through small animation, not decorative flourish.
- The `SubagentBubble` as an AI activity affordance: animated step cycling communicates "an agent is doing work," while the robot-to-chevron hover gives a progressive disclosure cue instead of a static icon.

**Portfolio angle:** this phase is the moment the prototype starts expressing the AI product, not just the shell. It also shows the early commitment to token naming and motion affordances.

**Evidence commits:** `b4e0ea7`, `37164cd`, `1635a08`, `52de801`.

## Portfolio Narrative Draft

I started with a small design playground and turned it into a full design-intent prototype for an AI app builder. The first commits were not throwaway setup; they were the foundation of the product grammar: exact sidebar dimensions, nav grouping, theme application, token naming, dark-mode behavior, and a review process that made visual decisions traceable.

The product then expanded surface by surface: notifications and template editing, role-based home states, permissions/settings/users, marketing pages, billing and credits, plan mode, workflow diagrams, direct edit controls, suggestions, onboarding, and marketplace publishing. The throughline was always the same: make AI-generated work understandable, reviewable, adjustable, and safe to hand off.

The core product bet was that AI builders should not jump straight from prompt to finished app. Users need trust artifacts: plans, diagrams, specs, previews, staged changes, cost signals, and audit trails. That idea shows up everywhere: Plan Mode before Build Mode, workflow diagram preview before app generation, ClarifyCards before ambiguous execution, WorkflowSpecCards as living artifacts in chat, and Direct Edit with save/discard instead of accidental destructive edits.

The deeper design work was not only visual. I defined editing models, state machines, component taxonomies, content rules, and handoff contracts. The Direct Edit research mapped the market and produced a two-surface model: floating toolbar for style, docked panel for component settings. The billing work turned credit consumption into a state machine with contextual recovery paths. The rebrand moved the product from Blinq to Pave through semantic tokens and accessibility-audited color choices. The marketplace and onboarding work shifted starting points from generic "templates" into live, inspectable, intent-first app previews.

The bigger system I built around the product is just as important as the screens. VibeDesign became a working design delivery environment with branch previews, preview banners, content guidelines, handoff docs, Mermaid diagrams, component annotations, token rules, and feature checklists. The result is a portfolio artifact that shows both product design craft and the operating system that made the craft repeatable.

Suggested portfolio framing:

- **Role:** Product designer / design engineer building an AI app-builder prototype and the design-delivery system around it.
- **Problem:** AI-generated apps are hard to trust unless users can inspect the plan, revise the logic, understand cost/risk, and safely edit the output.
- **Approach:** Build a runnable prototype that expresses intent through code, tokens, diagrams, motion, content guidelines, research-backed interaction models, and handoff docs.
- **Outcome:** A multi-surface product story covering onboarding, workflow generation, diagram/spec review, direct edit, billing/credits, marketplace governance, branch-preview review, and implementation handoff.
